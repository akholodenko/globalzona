SAMANTHA MUMBA

When you hear, "Here comes another teen pop star," what do you think? Another Britney clone? Someone who's going to try to mimic Christina's vocal range or Mandy's wholesome pop persona? Well, think again!

Samantha Mumba is definitely not your typical teenage pop star. This 17-year-old singer definitely speaks her mind and makes her own choices. For example, she thinks Kylie Minoque was always a cool pop star and cell phones are indispensable - she is a teenager after all! She sent her 30-something stylist home so she can pick out her own clothes! Would those other pop princesses ever dare to make their own choices like that?

"I'm black and I'm from Ireland," Mumba said. "It's kind alike, uh-oh…but it means I'm something totally different. There are a lot of female artists my age around at the moment, but they're all American and blonde and blue-eyed and smiley. I'm totally the opposite of that. I want to show a bit more attitude and I have an opinion…."

Mumba left school last year to start working on her exciting record deal. She was busy performing on stages and in studios for the previous six months that she would arrive at school already exhausted. Her exemplary academic record showed that she does commit whole-heartedly to whatever she does. Mumba made the tough decision to leave school, however, and focus full-time on her career. She says she'll rerun to school "if it all went quiet" with her career.

By the way things look, nothing is about to quiet down about her career anytime soon. Her songs, which she co-writes, are a confident collection of pop-influenced R&B tunes that prove this European talent can match the slick, catchy efforts of TLC, Brandy and Britney Spears.

In her native Dublin, Mumba has already become a local celebrity. She landed a lead role in a September 1998 production of the Gilbert and Sullivan opera "The Hot Mikado." She sang on various Irish TV shows - singing live every time, even when she was asked to lip synch.

Mumba claims that everybody knows everybody in Dublin. That worked out well for her, when a friend introduced her to Louis Walsh, manager of Boyzone and Westlife, in a Dublin club one night. She lied a bit when she told the manager that she was an R&B singer from New York who was recording her first album. This impressed Walsh, who signed her up to his roster of artists.

After signing to Polydor, Mumba spent several months traveling between Denmark, Sweden, England and Ireland, co-writing and recording her debut album Gotta Tell You. Although her songs are usually upbeat and pop-flavored, her music belies her tender years. "I've always had friends who were a couple of years older," she explains. Or as Britney would say, she's not that innocent.

Mumba grew up with Bryan McFadden from Westlife. They attended Dublin's Billie Barry Stage School together. "We'd probably have been the least expected to make it out of everybody, " she says. "He was a joker, and I was a dosser - always having a laugh, not taking it seriously either."

Of course, the final joke is now on everybody else. Samantha Mumba is the all-singing, all-dancing, all-real pop princess in waiting. You can't help but take her very seriously .
